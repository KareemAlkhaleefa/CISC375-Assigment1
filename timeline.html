Chess History Timeline

From ancient India to artificial intelligence — fifteen centuries of the royal game

Milestones in Chess History

The history of chess spans more than 1,500 years, crossing continents and cultures, evolving through the courts of emperors and the computers of engineers. The timeline below traces the most significant turning points in the game's extraordinary journey from a war simulation in ancient India to the most widely played strategy game in the world.

Historical Milestones

Ancient India

~600 AD

Chaturanga — The Birth of Chess

Chess emerges in the Gupta Empire of northern India as Chaturanga, a Sanskrit word meaning "four divisions" — the four branches of the ancient Indian army: infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots. Played on an 8×8 grid called the ashtāpada, the game's objective is to defeat the opposing king, a principle that will endure unchanged across fifteen centuries. Chaturanga is considered the earliest ancestor of all chess variants played worldwide.

Persia

~650 AD

Shatranj — Chess Enters Persia

Chess travels westward into the Sasanian Persian Empire, where it is renamed Shatranj. The Persians adapt the pieces and rules to their own culture and begin documenting game positions in written texts — the earliest chess theory. Elite players, called aliyat, are celebrated at the courts of Persian nobility. The Persian exclamation shāh māt — "the king is helpless" — will eventually give the world the word "checkmate."

Islamic World

~700–900 AD

Chess Spreads Through the Islamic Caliphates

Following the Arab conquest of Persia, chess sweeps across the expanding Islamic world at remarkable speed, from Baghdad to North Africa and the Iberian Peninsula. Arab scholars produce the first systematic chess treatises, cataloguing openings, tactical patterns, and endgame techniques. The 9th-century master al-Adli writes the earliest known chess book, establishing traditions of problem composition and positional analysis that will influence European players for centuries to come.

Medieval Europe

~1000 AD

Chess Reaches Europe

Chess enters Christian Europe through multiple routes: via the Moors in Spain, through trade contacts in Sicily, and along crusader pilgrimage roads. By the year 1000, the game is played across the courts of England, France, Germany, and Scandinavia. The Lewis Chessmen — stunning ivory figures carved around 1150–1200 AD and discovered in Scotland — become one of the most evocative symbols of medieval European chess culture. Chess becomes closely associated with knightly virtue and aristocratic education.

Renaissance

1475

The Modern Rules Emerge

A revolution in chess rules occurs in Spain and Italy: the queen — previously one of the weakest pieces — becomes the most powerful on the board. Bishops gain their long diagonal range, pawns can now advance two squares on their first move, and the en passant rule is introduced to compensate. These changes transform chess from a slow, strategic pastime into a fast, tactical game of combinations and sacrifices. The new rules spread across Europe through printed chess manuals, among the first books published on the continent.

19th Century

1851

The First International Tournament — London

The first international chess tournament is held in London in 1851, organized alongside the Great Exhibition. German master Adolf Anderssen wins the event, cementing his reputation as the world's finest player. The tournament establishes the modern competitive format and introduces chess to a mass international audience. Anderssen's legendary "Immortal Game" — played informally during the same event against Lionel Kieseritzky — becomes one of the most celebrated games in chess history, featuring a spectacular queen sacrifice.

19th Century

1886

First Official World Chess Championship

The first officially recognized World Chess Championship is contested between Wilhelm Steinitz of Austria and Johannes Zukertort of Germany, played across New York, St. Louis, and New Orleans. Steinitz wins convincingly, becoming the game's first undisputed World Champion. More significantly, Steinitz establishes the theoretical foundations of positional chess — arguing that advantages must be accumulated gradually, that pawn structure matters, and that pieces need open lines and strong squares. His ideas transform chess from romantic intuition to scientific discipline.

20th Century

1972

Fischer vs. Spassky — The Match of the Century

American prodigy Bobby Fischer challenges reigning World Champion Boris Spassky of the Soviet Union in Reykjavík, Iceland. Framed against the backdrop of the Cold War, the match captures global attention unlike any chess event before or since. Fischer wins 12½–8½, becoming the first — and to date only — American World Chess Champion. His demolition of the Soviet chess machine, which had dominated world chess for decades, is hailed as one of the greatest individual sporting achievements of the 20th century.

Computer Era

1997

Deep Blue Defeats Kasparov

IBM's chess computer Deep Blue defeats reigning World Champion Garry Kasparov in a six-game match in New York, winning 3½–2½. It is the first time a reigning World Champion has lost a match to a computer under standard tournament conditions, and marks a watershed moment in both chess and computing history. Kasparov, who had defeated an earlier version of Deep Blue in 1996, disputes the result and demands a rematch that IBM refuses. The victory signals the beginning of the computer age in chess: engines will eventually surpass all human players and fundamentally reshape how chess is studied and played.

Modern Era

2013

Magnus Carlsen Becomes World Champion

Norwegian prodigy Magnus Carlsen defeats five-time World Champion Viswanathan Anand in Chennai, India, claiming the World Chess Championship at the age of 22. Carlsen — who had held the world number-one ranking since 2010 and achieved a peak FIDE rating of 2882, the highest in history — ushers in a new era of chess dominance. He will successfully defend the title four more times before voluntarily relinquishing it in 2023. His decade-long reign, combined with the global chess boom catalysed by the COVID-19 pandemic and the Netflix series The Queen's Gambit, makes chess more popular than at any point in its long history.
